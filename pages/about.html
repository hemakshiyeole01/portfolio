The person behind
the code

This isn't a resume. It's the story of how a curious person from Nagpur started building things that matter — and felt of her intrest.

Hi, I'm Hemakshi.

I'm a third-year undergraduate studying Computer Science and Data Science at RCOEM, Nagpur. I'm the kind of person who gets genuinely excited when a messy dataset starts to tell a story — or when a rough idea becomes a system that actually works.

Engineering, for me, has never been just about writing code. It's about asking the right question first. What's actually broken here? Who uses this? What would make it feel obvious? I carry those questions into every project I touch — whether it's a Kafka pipeline processing live financial data or an accessible e-learning platform for a community that most students don't think about.

"I want to build things that matter — not just things that work."

Technically, I'm strongest in Java, NLP, and data engineering. I've shipped real systems: a streaming sentiment analysis pipeline, a legal case similarity engine using sentence transformers, a full-stack drone mission planner with PostGIS spatial queries, and an RL environment where an LLM acts as an emergency incident commander. Not toy projects — real, running systems with real technical decisions behind them.

I'm not only a developer.

In parallel to shipping code, I've been the Director of Creatives for Rotaract Club RBU — leading a team responsible for all visual communication, social media, merchandise, and event branding. I design T-shirts, banners, and social campaigns that actually get engagement, not just views.

I've also served as an NSS Executive, coordinating community outreach programs across Nagpur, and I've anchored campus events in front of large audiences. I mention all of this not to pad a CV — but because I believe the best engineers are also great communicators, and I've deliberately invested in that side of things.

I speak English, Hindi, and Marathi fluently. I can write clearly, present confidently, and explain a technical decision to a non-technical audience without being condescending about it.

What I believe in

These aren't values I picked off a list. They're things I've noticed showing up consistently in how I approach work.

🔍
Curiosity over comfort
I pick up tools I don't know yet, break them, and figure out why. The uncomfortable problem is almost always the interesting one.
🛠️
Shipping over perfecting
A working system teaches you more than a perfect design doc. I'd rather build v0.1 and iterate than wait for the right moment.
🧩
Clarity in complexity
Good engineering is making hard things simple to reason about. I care about architecture that future-me (or a teammate) can understand.
🤝
Empathy in design
Systems are used by humans. I think about who's on the other end of every API, every UI, every decision.
📣
Communication as craft
Technical skill without communication is half a skill. I invest in writing, presenting, and explaining well.
🌱
Compounding effort
CGPA 9.15 across five semesters while shipping projects and leading teams isn't luck — it's what consistency looks like over time.
